Chapter Nine

HANDLING CRITICISMS

If you really intend to be a great leader, prepare ahead for confrontations, attacks, criticisms and the like, for they will surely come. Diligence in leadership does not make you free from being critiqued. No matter how diligent and committed you are, still expect that some particular group of your followers will criticize some of your actions.

To conclude that everyone will accept you because you have done enough good for your organization is a costly assumption. Every leader, (and in fact everyone) who has ever dared to break into uncharted waters has faced criticisms, new problems and new challenges, but only the few who persevered ended up victorious.

There is nothing you do under heaven that can free you from criticism. You only learn to survive it. Criticism goes with leadership. Every leader, good or bad will be criticized. Why? This is because you simply cannot please everyone! What some will criticize you for, others will celebrate you for the same. What some will like about you, others will hate about you.

That is life for you. Not everyone will like your face, or your guts, or your ideas, or your accomplishments. This may sound unrealistic, but it is the truth. So, expect it. It is inevitable. Some of those criticisms may even come for reasons that may have little or nothing to do with you personally or your leadership. Accept them in good faith.

In the real sense of it, as stones are thrown at only fruit-bearings trees, criticism comes mainly to the efficient leaders. If you are a functional leader, do not be scared of it. A load of criticism will be constantly dumped on your personality, ego and projects. You will receive more attacks than your colleagues who are not half as committed as you are. Your achievements and the records you have laid will attract a lot of it.

Accept this fact early enough! Not every student will like you or your leadership style. No leader in history ever succeeded in having 100% followership. Even Jesus, the greatest man that

ever lived, had his enemies. It is natural that some youths fall headlong for you while some kick against every one of your moves. Jesus, as good as he was, and still is, had enough people who kicked against his leadership style, but this did not deter him.

Leaders who are afraid of criticism do not achieve much. Take a stand for the truth, critic or no critic. That some are against your leadership style and criticize every one of your action does not mean you have failed as a leader, neither does it mean you have to change your approach to leadership, especially when it is obvious you are on the right path. You cannot please everyone. The only likely way to avoid criticism is to attempt nothing and just maintain the status quo, but even at that, some people will still complain of your inactions!

The critics can choose any method of approach they deem fit. The bold ones may approach you face to face, while the cautious ones may try to avoid a direct contact with you and prefer to call, text, mail, or write. The very bad ones will go behind you telling tales around.

TYPES OF CRITICS

At times, criticism may seem to come at you from all sides, and sometimes, as ridiculous as it may sound, these criticisms will always come (1) from the least expected sources, (2) at the least expected time, and (3) it will be about the least expected issues. If not, you will not be so bothered about them. This is why you need to try as much as possible to live your life above reproach.

Do not be over-dependent on people. If you do, someday, someone will let you down. Expect disappointment from

anyone. Those you think will always be there for you may be the first to turn their backs on you.

Do not fully base your morale each time on people’s approval of your activities only. If you seek to please everyone every time, for fear of attack or rejection, you will soon get into problems with everyone. Most times, you will have to take a rigid stand and make up your mind to do what is right. Let your critics say or do whatever they like, as long as you are doing what is expected of you, the end result will justify your means.

There are two classes of critics you will find among your people:

The first are those who assess your performance and encourage you to continue by showing you areas in which you need to put in more effort. These ones are calm and inviting. Their words may be tough on you, but their intention is to see you become a better leader. Some of them may even be violent about it. Look beyond their action to their argument, and you will discover that what they lack is just a comfortable way of presenting their arguments; they are still on your side. These ones are called constructive critics.

The second are those who really do not have to assess you before they conclude that you are not fit to be the leader. Even when they have to necessarily carry out an official evaluation of your performance, their result is always biased, since all they want is to discredit your administration. These ones are always very violent, their arguments lopsided, and their conclusions destructive to both your personality and your leadership. You really need to know how to handle them or they may cause

catastrophic shake-ups in the group in order to destabilize things for you. These ones are called destructive critics.

Be careful! Do not make the mistake of taking the first for the second. Learn how to differentiate the constructive ones from the destructive ones. The difference is not always in their manner of approach, but in their end point. One is to see you become a better leader; the other is to make sure you are disgraced out of office.

APPRECIATE YOUR CRITICS

Do not be hasty in dismissing the allegations of your critics. Whether the first or the second group, never ignore criticism when you are wrong, and never fight it when you are right. Maintain a matured leader’s attitude.

View your critics as your friends and not your enemies. Take each criticism seriously. An honest evaluation of their criticism will show you the degree of attention you need to give to some of the issues they are bothered about.

There are two types of friendship you will have among your followers: (1) The sheep in wolves’ clothing and (2) The wolves in sheep clothing. You will agree the former is better than the latter; and that’s the group where your critics fall.

In a sense, you should show sincere appreciation to those who criticize you openly, whether constructively or destructively. Some of the people you trust say worse things in your absence and smile in your presence. These are wolves in sheep clothing. So, do not take any serious offence in those who criticize you often, they may not know it, but they are doing you the favor

which most of your friends and fans will not do – opening your eyes to your shortcomings. Somehow, we may call them the sheep in wolves clothing.

Your best teachers are your destructive critics if you know how to learn from them. They will paint you the way you really are or worse. They may, most times, overemphasize some details to their own advantage, but you can be sure they are saying some salient truths, most of which some of your intimate friends will hide from you intentionally, probably so as not to embitter or derail you.

This is not to say all criticisms are right; but it is more dangerous to conclude all are wrong.

HOW TO MINIMIZE CRITICISMS

Critiques cannot be completely eliminated. You will always have them around you. However, you can silence them or make them irrelevant by taking some conscious steps. These steps are explained below.

1. CRITICISE YOURSELF

Do not wait until your critiques spur you into action before you get into action. In order to minimize criticisms from others, first criticize yourself by all standards.

Do a constant self-evaluation. Self-criticisms prevent public criticisms. If you can genuinely assess your own processes and performances frankly and justly, and you are willing to make amendments where necessary, you will leave little or nothing for your critiques to talk about.

Criticism is the rod with which God chastises and corrects erring leaders. You either allow Him to correct you using your own hands through self-criticism, or let him go through your followers using open criticism. You can be sure one is better than the other.

2. ENCOURAGE POSITIVE CRITICISMS

Intentionally invite criticisms. Encourage the contribution of your people in the assessment of your performance. If you have taken the previous step, which is self-evaluation, you will have nothing to fear asking for their assessment.

Let them know you will appreciate constructive criticisms and create an avenue for them to express it. You may give them a phone number, an e-mail address, or place a suggestion box in a public place for those who care.

Also, schedule periodic Question and Answer sessions for people who have serious and genuine complaints. You can even make appointments with some of them, individually, so they can air their views. This way, you have beaten them to it.

3. DO NOT CRITICIZE OTHERS NEGATIVELY

Do not be a destructive critic of another. If your followers discover that you are fond of criticizing others, they will be encouraged to do the same about you.

If you have issues with any of your followers, or with another leader, rather than spend reasonable time spreading untrue tales about the person, or condemning his activities, call the person aside and discuss your grievances constructively.

This will, in a way, save the person from public shame, and he will be grateful to you. It will also serve as a lesson to him, and numerous other onlookers. They have unconsciously learnt how you want to be treated if others have issues with you.

As a rule, if you are a leader, never spend your time telling tales about others. People who criticize others to you will criticize you to others.

ANSWERING YOUR CRITICS

When asked questions by any of your critics, do not rush to give any answers. Take your time; respond slowly. Think through what you want to say before you open your mouth. If it is from a destructive critic, the questions may be a trap to get you to say what you should not say.

Criticisms, no matter how well meaning they may be, are not palatable. If you are ready for them, make up your mind to receive whatever they have to say in whatever package they offer it. Do not expect your critics to speak nice words. They are, most times, unkind in their choice of words. Some of them may really not intend to hurt your feelings, but they end up doing exactly that without knowing it.

Criticism is different from insults. “Insults” have nothing good to say about you or your services and can be unreasonably abusive without listening to any explanation, but criticism, negative or positive, will open your eyes to your short comings. This is because critics have points. They assess you and your performance and reveal your weakness to all and the manner of presentation of their assessments is not always pleasant.

Although their conclusions after assessing you may be wrong, and they may also be right, it does not matter to them.

If you have to answer a critic, do not answer when you are angry. First calm down, cool your temper, then you can give an answer.

Constructive or destructive, take the criticisms without being crushed or defeated. Never think of retaliating negative criticisms. Do not fire back at them. Constant encounter with such, mainly immature followers and their unfair criticisms may be frustrating. Do not get personal. Do not be defensive.

The basic intention of most of your critics is to hurt you. This they will do in three ways;

(1) by hurling hurtful and biting words at you;

(2) by spreading bad and untrue news about you;

(3) by instigating you to overreact and do or say things you should not.

Once they can successfully use the first two to make you do the third one, they have achieved their aims. Do not be moved by the tone of their words or letters. Be yourself; do not let their plans see the light of day.

It is a known fact that youths generally are volatile, but this statement is truer than ever today. For this reason, you will need to be careful in your choice of words when replying. Avoid being offensive in defending your points. Words have different meanings to different people. Some words instigate youths and make them want to foment trouble while other choice of words can pacify their violent spirits. Know when to use which.

Youths rarely appreciate good leaders when they see one, hence they hardly get one. If you are one, keep being good. Your deeds may not be appreciated now, but the end will justify the means. Those who have turned against you will come back to seek your favor.

Assess their criticism. Evaluate their barbs and gibes. If their point is justifiable, try to correct it, but if it is illegitimate, forget it as quick as possible.

WHEN WILL YOU BE CRITICIZED?

Expect criticism anytime and every time your critics are opportune to build a case, major or minor, against you. Youths will use every opportunity they have to criticize your actions, especially if your tenure is not favorable to them. Every time your actions do not go down well with them, you will be opposed.

Below are four major periods during which you cannot avoid their criticism if you are a leader.

1. BEFORE INSTALLATION

They will start criticizing you even before the installation. They will fault either the electioneering process or the selection technique by which you emerged the leader. They will fault the king makers and the members of the electoral committee for not being fair during the process. They are so sure you are the wrong choice. You will do yourself good not to listen to them. Your success during your leadership will prove them wrong.

2. DURING INSTALLATION

Some may still continue their ministry during the installation. They will condemn the process of installations because it was not done according to their expectation. They will condemn the program of events, condemn the organizing committee, condemn the speeches, condemn the officials present and condemn the leader to be installed. Some of them may even condemn the type of meal that was served at the installation ceremony when there is nothing more to condemn, as though the food is more important to them than the person being installed. All of these are none of your problem. You just face the reason why you are there – to be installed as the leader.

3. AFTER INSTALLATION

Do not think they are through with you yet. They have just started. Every one of your program will be criticized. If you do something, you will be criticized. If you do nothing, they still will criticize your procrastinations. If you shout, they will say you are too harsh. If you are mute, they will say you are too weak. If you manage the purse well, they will say you are stingy. If you spend lavishly, they will say you are too extravagant.

Not all will approve of your style of leadership. Some will feel you are too sophisticated or contemporary. Some may even feel you are too canal, while others may feel you are too spiritual. Your followers will always have divergent views of your leadership style. Adaptability and the view will be dependent on who is doing the evaluation. All through the period of your tenure, expect to be criticized.

4. AFTER THE TENURE

Now they have enough reasons to criticize you since you have handed the baton of power over to your successor. Everything you are supposed to do which you did not do will be criticized. The ones you are not supposed to do that you did will also be criticized. Even the ones you did, but by their judgment, were not well done, will also be criticized. They will review every one of your actions and look for enough proofs to convince everyone that you are the worst leader they ever had.

WHO WILL CRITICIZE YOU?

You will encounter pressures from many fronts, most of which will combat you with the intent to destroy your good works. Be prepared for this.

You must readily expect insults and criticism from six groups of people.

1. YOUR OPPONENTS

Your opponents will never have anything good to say about you. They want you out of the office so they can take charge by planting their own candidate there, and they will use every possible means to achieve their goal.

They really are not interested in your good works, it matters little to them. What they pray for and are on the lookout for is that you commit blunders so they can nail you and kick you out.

So, whatever insult or criticism you receive from them, handle same with utmost care. Do not try to buy them over, or try to win them to yourself; it may work for some of them for a while but not all of them at every time. Just be on your own and face

your official duty with a clean plate such that they will have no hold on you.

If you give them a chance to get at you, you are a goner!

2. YOUR WORK FORCE

You will be shocked to hear this! Your work force, who should be the most supportive, will have enough reasons to criticize you once in a while. Do not be disappointed. The purpose of their criticism is different; it is, most times, not intended to pull you down. It is to see you become a better leader.

So, you must desire to hear more of their criticisms. The concern of your work force is legitimate since your success is their success. So, do not be intimidated by their constant constructive criticisms. The earlier you sit down, examine their criticisms and differentiate them from others, the more of these you will want.

None of them is going to overthrow your leadership. If you truly gained their allegiance and trust as soon as you entered the office, without stepping on their toes, they will not turn their backs on you. So, take their criticism as precious words of advice.

3. FELLOW LEADERS

New levels attract new devils and fresh oppositions come with new position. One of the oppositions you will face will come from other leaders. You are not the only leader in your church or around the neighborhood. There are several other leaders leading other segments, units, hostels, departments, faculties and fellowships.

Your commitment to your leadership responsibilities and your uncommon achievements may begin to generate jealousy among them. If you are really doing what is expected of you as much as you are supposed to, you are bound to be hated, because your commitment to your tasks will make it obvious that some of the others are really not doing as much as is expected of them. Your achievements compared to theirs will serve as a proof of this.

Once other followers begin to compare their leaders’ achievements to yours, they begin to notice the flaws in their leaders and then begin to condemn them. This will definitely make the other leaders around hate you. Since they cannot remove you of their own accord, they will begin to criticize you. They will criticize every one of your actions so as to get back at you. This is a battle every leader must learn to fight!

4. INTIMATE FRIENDS

Your intimate friends too will criticize you. The friends you had before getting to the leadership position still expect to have as much access to you now as they had then. They still want to have as much hold on you.

They may want to direct you and push you around because of the familiarity. If you must carry out your duties successfully, such should not be encouraged. They still remain your friends, but they must be made to understand that official matters are not supposed to be sacrificed on the altar of acquaintance.

This is where the problem lies. Most of them will not want to see it this way. They will immediately conclude that you are now proud, and then start to criticize all of your actions.

Unlike others, these ones are easy to deal with. Simply call their attention to the obvious, they will understand and let you be.

You really do not have to please everyone because you definitely cannot. The easiest way to encounter failure as a leader is by trying to please everybody. Let such friends know you cannot always dance to their tunes.

5. YOUR FOLLOWERS

Beside your opponents and fellow leaders, the strongest of the criticism you will experience will come from your followers. Do not expect every youth around to praise you.

Irrespective of how much you have achieved, it is possible that most will not appreciate whatever you have done. Some honestly will; but most will be too unwilling to give credit to you for your achievements. That is youths for you.

Youths are the most difficult people to lead. They are not too easy to please, and the reason is simply because they rarely own their own thought. They do not reason with their own minds. They act on someone else’s thought and reasoning. They move and react on someone else’s orders.

When a majority leader says you are good, they start to praise you. The day he decides to turn his back on you; they all start to condemn your actions. They are like the Jews who hailed Jesus on a Sunday morning saying “Hosanna to the son of David. Blessed is He that cometh in the name of the Lord” but the following Friday screamed “Crucify Him”.

As difficult as it looks, these ones are easy to handle. Since their criticism is as a result of misconception of your actions, you only need to sponsor a silent campaign that will address the issue. This way, you will be able to silent the opposition who is sponsoring the crisis.

6. THE HIGHER AUTHORITY

The last group you will receive criticism from is the higher authority; but this will only happen if you are not doing the right thing. If you are on the right path, you seldom will have any problem with them. The higher authority will include the leader of the mother church, the school authority, and the other leaders around to whom you are answerable.
