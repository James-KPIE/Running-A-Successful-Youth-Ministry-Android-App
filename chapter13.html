Chapter Thirteen

EFFECTING A CHANGE

Today’s youth leader will not function effectively with methods and mindsets of yesterday. He will need to review the approach, style, and methods he meets on ground and ascertain whether they are relevant or not and see if there is need for either an upgrade or a change. Trying to use the exact same method and skill, which your predecessor used without much success and hoping to get a better result, is foolhardy. You need to adjust your thinking; you need to see things from a different perspective.

Do a process evaluation of all of the programs and projects you meet on ground. Ask yourself this all important question: “Has this method been productive, time saving, and cost effective?” If the answer to the three is yes, you really may not have it changed except you think there are better alternatives that will help achieve the same result in a better way (and be prepared to face serious opposition from others for that). However, if your answer is no, then you can begin to think of change.

Do not be afraid to upgrade to new standards of excellence. Irrespective of human opposition, every tradition that wants to hinder you from implementing the necessary changes that are

for the good of the organization must be addressed on time. What is keeping you from making these necessary changes? Is it funds, materials, the youths themselves, the school authority, church authority or environmental factors? Disregard the resistance; stand against the opposition. Maintain your stand and initiate the needed change. Everyone will be the better for it someday.

As a means of starting up the crusade for change; you can ask your workforce to answer in a written form the question “What in this organization needs to change? Answering verbally may reduce the impact of the question and water down its effect on them but giving them as a take home assignment will task their minds and engage their thoughts in a silent review of all the projects at hand such that it will be too obvious to the majority that desire changes what needs to be changed. This done, your task of convincing them becomes simplified.

However, be careful. Do not just agree to change everything everyone feels needs changing. Take your time to critically examine each process before initiating a move. Make sure there is a very cogent reason for introducing the change which every one of your followers desire, and that there is a very urgent need for it. Then, the change can be justified. The reason for the change should not just be for the sake of having your name written on people’s minds. It must have a purpose - to make life better for your followers.

THE RISK OF CHANGE

After deciding on what to change, prepare the minds of your followers for the change to expect. You do not expect them to make abrupt changes to what they have been doing over the

years. They are humans and not robots. You will need a lot of persuasion in order to get their full cooperation. Unless you are able to successfully persuade them, your dream of creating a change may be unrealized.

You would have thought that present day youths, because of their exposure, would be readily willing to accept positive changes; but this is not so. Most times, they do not find it that easy to adapt to changes too, except when they are seriously persuaded, tricked or forced to. They see a re-examination of their methods, styles, and principles as shifting base from what used to be obtainable to a strange one. The fear of the unknown will make them want to keep on doing things the way they did before your tenure.

Change involves a high level of risk! Trying to initiate a major change in a youth populated organization without adequate preparation for it could be disastrous. You will need to be sensitive enough to understand the in-depth feelings of all involved. If the change you are initiating comes out successfully at the end of the day, you will be esteemed and remembered for life. However, if for whatever reasons, it turns out the other way round, you may have your image tarnished for life and it may take sometime before anyone asks you to come lead any other youth organization again. So, be certain about the need for, the purpose of, and to a large extent, the success of the change you want to initiate ever before you start the process. Be guaranteed of success before taking off.

THE STAKE HOLDERS OF CHANGE

For any change to be effective, there are some people whose support you do not need and there are some you cannot do

without. Know which is which. To mistake the former for the latter may not be too healthy for the change you are canvassing for. Therefore, you will need to be certain you have the support of those in whose absence the change cannot materialize.

Spear heading change is difficult. This is not just among youths; it is all over. This is because every great idea that requires that people change their way of life always generates conflicts at the initial stage. Endeavor to ascertain that all those to be affected by the change are considered and contacted before the change is effected.

One of the first sets of people to be put into consideration are the initiators of the programs you intend to change. Every change you propose is always a critique of what is obtainable in the past. So, if you must do it successfully, you do not have to openly condemn the past leaders or their works. Apply wisdom.

Remember, some of them still have some proponents in the organization who will report your bitterness towards them frequently. This may generate serious problems for you in your pursuit of change. If they have done well, be grateful to them and praise them openly so as to garner enough support for yourself, both from them and from their advocates. If they have not done well, keep your mouth shut about it and face your own business, bearing in mind that your main business is not to criticize the past leadership, but to take the group forward. This is not politics, it is leadership.

There is nothing as devastating to a new leader as being criticized by his predecessors openly. This will erode the little support and trust which others have in him. To ensure that this

does not happen, do not criticize your predecessors openly. Treat them specially. Carry them along if need be. If you are creating a change that has to do with the foundation any of them laid, and you feel the person in question is still very relevant to the group, or has a strong influence on it, inform them ahead of the change. Let them first hear from you before they hear from external sources. Involve them in problems that relate to them. Get their opinions about these. Ask them why they did what they did and let them know why you want to do what you want to do. Let them see reason with you. You do not have to get their 100% consent, just carry them along. Carrying them along in this regard simply means letting them know what you want to do and why you want to do it.

For the change agent, change comes with its own agony; be ready for your share. Another of such agony you will have to contend with is your followers. Never expect everyone to support your change.

Often times, if your followers are like other youths, if your strategies are not well planned, the ones you think should embrace the change will be the first to resist it. This is not enough reason to give up on what you know you should do. It is like that everywhere.

Two major factors are responsible for this:

(1) Fear of the Unknown

As outgoing as they are, most youths are still very reserved when it comes to initiating changes. They are more comfortable with the devil they know than trust an angel they know not. They prefer to use their own time-tested, proven programs, than

jump at new ones. Even if it is so obvious that the group is long due for the change, some youths will still be skeptical about it. No matter what you say, they will not see the prospect of the change you are introducing. What they only focus on is the disadvantages if any, or the inconvenience of the period of change. They will rather stay in their comfort zone than explore virgin lands.

(2) Loss of Privileges and Authority

Not everyone gains from change. No matter how good the change may be, some youths will lose either their relevance in the group or their grip on some privileged opportunities if the change is permitted. These youths who are beneficiaries of the old order and are sure not to get such esteemed benefits in the new order will fight it to a standstill with all their might because they are going to experience some visible loss if such change is finally effected.

There is no use pretending the change you are about to bring will benefit all concerned. This, most likely, will not be the complete truth. Such pretence will cause you to ignore the passive protest of those who will really need to make personal or corporate adjustments in order for the change to be effective. If you neglect these ones, they will summon courage to boldly challenge you and see to it that you have difficulty succeeding in your bid, if at all you do. They will amplify the detriments of the change such that it will overshadow its benefits. You will be too shocked that the majority of the youths will listen to them, rather than to you. They always shield one another’s backs.

These ones are easily noticeable; you cannot miss them! Their main question is always “What is wrong with the way we used to

do it? Why do we have to change it? Once you identify any of them, learn how to handle them maturely because the success of the change you are about to initiate, to an extent, depends on them.

In leadership, from time to time, you will definitely be forced to step on some other people’s toes. It is inevitable, but the fewer they are, the better. Stepping on their toes may not necessarily be because their toes are along the path of the fulfillment of your dream. It could be because the path you are creating is along the route their toes have always been. These people need to be attended to when their toes are stepped on, either intentionally or unconsciously.

Learn to handle both the gains and the losses of change. The ability to accommodate both those who support and those who criticize your actions and programs is an indispensable virtue at times like this. Listen to every reasonable critic and carefully evaluate them. Some of the best changes you will make will be made after listening to some of your opponents and responding honestly to their claims.

Sit them down and talk with them one on one. Come to their individual levels. Make them see reason why the change is long overdue and necessary at a time like this. Appreciate their concern and allay their fears. Count their losses (at least for a few months after the change is introduced) and make up for these by integrating them into an aspect of your program.

However, whatever aspect of your program you want to compensate them with must be profitable enough to make up for a larger percentage of their losses but must not be visible

enough to impact your program negatively if they decide to turn their backs on you midway. Such youths cannot be fully trusted; they may stage an open rebellion, a quiet resistance, or a subtle sabotage. So, be careful with them until you are convinced of their allegiance.

RIGHT TIMING FOR CHANGE

There is a particular time that is very right for every change. The cause of the failure of most leaders who have canvassed for change is that of inaccurate timing; either the change came too soon before it was needed or later than it was needed.

It is very easy to be carried away by the certainty of the success which will result from the change you desire to make without bothering your head about its time. You will need to be certain that the time is right for it. This is because it is possible to want to do the right thing at the wrong time and vice versa.

Ask yourself honestly:

* Is this change needed RIGHT NOW?

* If it is not done now, will there be any grievous consequence?

* Can this change be delayed? Why?

* Should it be hastened? Why?

* Can it be abandoned or annulled? Why?

* Should it be a temporal change or a permanent one?

* What has the organization been losing in the absence of this change?

* What does it stand to gain if it is initiated?

* What will be the people’s initial reaction to the change?

* Will their attitude change with time, after seeing the prospects of this change?

* What happens if the followers do not like the change, even after seeing the prospects?

* How easy is it to revert back to the status quo if there is need for it?

The questions above, if honestly answered, will enable you determine if the time you are proposing for the change is ideal and if your followers are ready for the impact of the change you want to spearhead.

THE OTHER SIDE OF CHANGE

Besides initiating changes, a leader must be ready to accept changes when necessary. There is no reason to be dogmatic. No one is perfect; everyone needs to change from time to time. An average leader only shows his willingness to change when the pressure is high. This is not the best.

If you see reasons why you must, why not? It is unethical to fight the voice of reasoning. If you expect them to change whenever change is required of them, you also must be ready to change whenever it is necessary. Do not be adamant in your operational style.
