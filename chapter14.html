Chapter Fourteen

DECISION MAKING

One major ability you cannot run away from is the ability to make the right decision. Everyone makes decision; and thousands of them, every day: what to eat, what to say, what to

put on, where to go. A leader makes much more important decisions than these on daily basis. He makes decisions that not only affect his own daily life, but the destinies of thousands of his followers as well.

One thing that will guarantee the failure of any leader is if he always makes up his mind on what to do without seeking anybody’s opinion. Such ideas become the leader’s idea, and the project, his project. He will either have to push it himself alone, or impose it on them, and he is likely to meet with hot resistance if he decides to do the latter. The resistance may either be active, or passive, depending on the group of youths he is leading.

Henry Ford, the great inventor, did not remember to put a reverse gear in his first auto mobile. This sounds ridiculous, but it also proves that no one is a know-it-all. In making decisions, despite the fact that you are still the leader, you may have to do a lot of consultations before arriving at a final standpoint. No matter how brilliant your ideas look, you will still need to subject it to the tight scrutiny of some sort of working committee before deciding to go ahead with it.

This is one of the greatest challenges facing youth leaders - decision making. There is nothing like a perfect idea. In order to make an idea a perfect one, it will require multiple touches of modifications. This is why there is need for a group decision-making. Always put up a team of trusted hands who will analyze the issues critically and help you make a quick decision that will favor the organization as a whole, and the individual members that build it up.

You do not have to make all the decisions. No single leader has all the required knowledge, the needed gifts, the necessary talents and the essential abilities that are mandatory for successful leadership. He will always need others. It is shown on the pages of history that men who got to leadership positions and never listened to other people’s advice always failed. Check around and you will see.

Dialogue must come before decision. Dialogue together with those who are relevant to the decision you are about to take. Let everyone present contribute their own quota. Even a mad man at times makes sense when listened to. Listen to everybody. Examine the situation from every possible perspective presented. Evaluate their objections and criticisms.

Respect the opinion of everyone in the meeting, although, this will in no sense mean that every opinion must be accepted. It also does not mean you have to carry out every suggestion; you do not. However, giving everyone the opportunity to speak will build a positive atmosphere and a team spirit. It also will allow you choose the best part of everybody’s contribution to build the best solution to the problem at hand.

At such meetings, your opinion is very important, so, do not ever make any uninformed decision. Throwing your support behind any issue on which you know close to nothing about, or which you know you will certainly lose the vote, will gradually reduce your influence among your followers.

Therefore, before you go in for any decision-making-meeting, be prepared. Do all your background work. Ensure you have all the needed facts and figures as regards all the issues you are

presenting to your audience. This will help you win their vote with ease.

Before making up your mind on any issue at the meeting, irrespective of the number of consultations you have made before the meeting, asking for the opinion of others in the meeting may give you a different outlook of the issue under discussion and you will be able to explore divergent views that were not previously considered.

Whether you are really sure of your convictions or not, ask for your team’s opinion before you form yours. Some of them need to be encouraged before they can speak out their minds; encourage them. One way to encourage those who speak out their minds is to let them know that they are appreciated and that their opinions are valuable.

The fact that the project sounds timely and laudable to you does not mean others will feel the same way. So, encourage open discussion. Hear them out at the meeting. If you do not, you will be seen as a leader who does only what he pleases, whenever he pleases, however it pleases him, and this will surely get you where you are not pleased to be.

As a leader, you must learn to listen with an open mind. Unless you want to single-handedly carry out the whole project, take the final decision with your team. No one is an island of wisdom. No one can think of every possible situation. No one knows it all. To be a great leader, you will need to get ideas from others, listen to their points of view, and make them suggest alternatives, even if they differ from what you intend.

It does not take long for youths to term their leader a dictator, especially if they feel he is the only one responsible for the generation of the idea and the decision-making. Youth leadership is not like a military regime where force can be applied to get things done. It is not a paramilitary college. It is either they willingly want to do it with you or they leave you to do it alone.

Actively involve your team members in decision making. You may already have a ready answer to the puzzle, but seeking their opinion will encourage their participation. Give them freedom to make their own decisions. This makes them feel like co-leaders, not laborers in another man’s vineyard.

At the point of decision making, no personal conflict must be allowed to affect the decisions which will be made. If care is not taken at such meetings, selfish motives will creep in and affect the quality of the decision to be made. Thus, in turn, will affect the house, adversely.

Do your best to avoid selfish interest and hidden agenda. You cannot have your way every time. Leadership is not all about making orders and decrees, neither is it about enforcing personal decisions using positional authority. It is about leading the group both in wise decision making, and being actively involved in the process of its accomplishment.

WHEN YOUR IDEAS ARE QUESTIONED

However relevant the project is to your followers, some may still question the merit of the project either because of their genuine interest in the group, or their selfish interest and personal gains.

Some of your advices may be rejected, some motions realigned and some of your ideas forcefully kicked against. As a good leader, you need to know how to handle such people responsibly, and also to handle your emotions when dealing with them or else you will blow things out of proportion.

Accept them all in good faith and in the spirit of good sportsmanship. Do not see these questionings as opposition or resistance to your leadership, especially if they are issue-based and not based on personal conflicts. That your team members always question you is not a sign of disagreement; they are indicating their need for better clarification. Do not get offended because of this. Your workforce are not looking for your down fall; so, trust their judgment.

Be willing to explain again and again until the last person or, at least, a majority of the people in the decision making committee understand your stand. Do not force anyone to agree; make them see reasons. Remember, if your tenure must be successful, you will need their cooperation, and to get their cooperation, you will need to win their heart. The fact that you have won their votes does not mean they will swear their 100% allegiance to you. They have a choice as to what to accept and what not to.

Although, it is greatly annoying when followers deliberately disagree with your opinion, especially with regard to issues which you have devoted time to think about and have arrived at a logical conclusion. However, if you will be willing to give them a chance to air their view, they will reveal their foolishness and shallow mindedness. It is then they will begin to appreciate the kind of leader they have and then argue less with you. If you

deny them this opportunity, you may be seen as a domineering leader, and the consequence, as discussed earlier will not be palatable.

In the event that they are wrong, you do not need to shower abusive language on them, showing them your mental prowess and thinking ability. Just explain carefully to them why you chose that alternative above others; and they will learn to respect your opinion next time. Then it will no longer be your sole decision; it will be the general decision of the house, and you will be loved the more for it. If you prefer to shout them down, the hurting party may take offence and gang up against you, frustrating all your efforts to make the idea succeed, even if the idea is a good one.

WHEN YOUR IDEAS ARE REJECTED

Alternatively, it may be the other way round. When your followers have aired their views, you may discover you are completely wrong, or at least, not as right as you thought you were. Your thoughtlessness, at such times, will be open to all.

At such times, your working committee may be forced to discard your idea for another. It will be unreasonable to expect that they will always embrace every proposal you present to them. Others too may have better options, and this may make them abandon your proposal for the better alternative.

This is in no way meant to spite you. Be not hard on them. Be courteous and kind to those who do not share your opinion. They are not against you, they are just willing to see the group grow, and this should gladden your heart.

Take it in good faith. Admit your errors and faults. Do not insist on the acceptance of your own ideas every time, especially when it is so obvious that there are better alternatives. Such attitude portrays immaturity on your part. Never impose your views on anyone. Be thoughtful of their opinions. Respect every one’s view as different as they may be from your own.

If the idea of the leadership is rejected, it does not mean the leader is rejected. They still love and want to have you as their leader. That is not debatable. Therefore, waste no time trying to defend your present position. It is not a sign of good leadership. A Yiddish proverb says “Protest long enough that you are right, and you will be wrong.”

You need not stubbornly stick to your old idea because you are the leader and the mantle of authority lies in your hand. Since it is now obvious to all that your idea is porous, and that there is a better way to go about it, trying to be defensive will not help matters. Building a self-defense only show that you are not ready to let go of your idea, irrespective of whatever is wrong with it.

Be quick to relax on the issue and do not press further. The case at hand is simple. Just admit that you missed out on some cogent point while assessing the situation (which in most cases may be the whole truth) and that was responsible for your initial choice. This is the evidence of good leadership. When you have to, acknowledge your methods are not as effective as you thought they would be. Assure them that you will be willing to shift grounds now that you have a clearer view of the situation at hand. This admission will in no way reduce you as a leader, neither will it make them look down on you. On the contrary, it

will portray you as a leader with a listening hear and a teachable spirit.

If given the chance, go back home and do your homework well. When you return with a better alternative, you will get a standing ovation.

To take undue advantage of your poor presentation, some others who do not flow readily with your leadership style and projects may also suggest ideas which you may not want to buy into. It will not make much sense to become argumentative with them or to take offence in them. You do not have to shout them down, everyone at the meeting already has an inclination that you are not in the best of terms with them. Doing this will confirm everybody’s fear that you are an unforgiving and wicked leader. Simply throw their idea on the table for general discussion. If you really have surrounded yourself with good brains as discussed under the chapter that addresses building your workforce, the idea itself will determine its own stand. If it is one that cannot hold water, it will melt away during critical assessment by the house without you making more enemies for yourself.

WHEN YOU ARE NOT TOO SURE

When you are ignorant about an issue or a topic, do not always be the first to speak. Accept guidance from others. Let your people debate the issue first among themselves until you get the whole idea. This should be done orderly.

Even when there is division in the house, work on them until there is a general agreement. Make sure everyone reaches a

consensus. Rather than argue against one another, let them brain storm, it is better this way.

As the leader, listen before you speak. Do not just listen; listen well and learn. Do not exercise zeal without knowledge. Delay all actions until you know what the issue is all about. Listen to them. Access all the sides of the story, weigh the pros & cons before you take a stand. Listen attentively while others bare their minds on the issue, else you will expose your folly.

Simply sum up the discussion at major intervals. Gather relevant facts and information, such that when you open your mouth to speak, your words will be laced with great wisdom. It is better to speak last and say words of wisdom than to speak hastily and later regret your action.

LAST WORD

In decision making, two things are key for the youth leader to be careful about:

1. Taking nobody’s advice

2. Taking everyone’s advice

The first is what we have discussed all through this chapter. The second is the exact opposite – taking everybody’s advice.

There is much wisdom in limiting decision making to only those involved in taking the decision and those whom the decision is going to affect directly. You do not have to sample everyone’s opinion if everyone is not going to be affected; you only need to carry them along.

Sampling everyone’s opinion may sound ideal, but if not well handled, especially in a large crowd, the choice of some youths’ opinion to the neglect of others may generate serious crisis among the rest of the youths.

You will definitely receive so many advices and counsels unsolicited. Some of them are needed, some of them are not. Some are out of pure hearts with the simple motive of moving you forward, others with selfish interests and hidden motives.

If the decision has nothing to do with a set of your followers, you only need to let them know why you are taking the decision and how it is going to make life better for the totality of them all. Let them know what you have gone through in taking the decision. Let them know you have contacted the parties involved and you have their consent. Unnecessarily involving people who are not relevant to the discussions and who are not stakeholders always makes decision making process unreasonably complicated.
